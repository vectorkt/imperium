Your guild and world


You are part of a small collection of inexperienced Planars, shareholders running a starting business on a relatively newly added world. The players are encouraged to cooperate in creating both their guild and the world before the actual game begins. Anything is possible, from banking, to adamantine prospecting, to mercenary company, on an ocean planet, jungle moon, or fiery world. The only rule is that no player may invalidate what another player has put forward. In short, the rule is called “Yes, and…”. Keep in mind that you will be playing with whatever you create, so any tomfoolery will reflect back on you. Shares in the company are represented by Tass, Tass being measured in chests. Each character brings 3 Tass to the table.


Occupations and Core Mechanic


Each character is defined by Occupations. If you can justify how an Occupation is relevant for the situation your character is in, you can use it. Each Occupation has a bonus value (1-10) called Rank. Rolling is simple. You roll a ten sided die, add bonuses and penalties, then compare it to a Target Number. If the Total Result is equal or higher, the character succeeds at his action. In short:


1d10 + Occupation Rank + Modifiers VS Target Number


When rolling a 1 or a 10, the die is rerolled, and the new result is kept. On a double 10, a character has performed a Critical Success (i.e. producing not only a painting, but the next Mona Lisa). On a double 1, a Character has a Critical Failure (the gun not only misses, but hits an ally).


A character begins with three Occupations: Background, Profession, and Hobby. These must be specific, e.g. if a character has a soldier Profession he must decide if he’s a Rifleman, Paratrooper, Navy Seal etc. It helps if they are defined as occupations and not skills, as they offer a wider usage. For example a charracter who has Hobby Architect might know a bit of the language of the workers on his construction site. Someone who only has Hobby Architecture will probably not.


After choosing his initial Occupations, the player must assign Ranks to each Occupation (starting set is +3,+2,+1 in any order preferred). Ranks specify exactly how good a character is on those associated Occupations. Note that Occupations don't stack, so if you have Military Sniper +2 as Background and Hunter +1 as Hobby your character will only be as good with a sniper rifle as his highest Occupation.


Sleeve


With powerful arcane arts come powerful conjuration and transmutation. Custom bodies become available, and some creatures are even grown and harvested for the particular reason of being inhabited by Planars. These custom bodies are known as Sleeves, and a Planar controls them through an amulet called a Phylactery, which stores an instance of the Planar’s mind. The Phylactery is somewhere on or inside the Slevee. A wide range of shapes, types, and sizes are available for Sleeves, from organics to mechanical.


Numerous guilds out there provide Sleeves for usage. A fresh new Sleeve comes with three important Traits: Life, Will, and Mana. These have a Maximum and an expendable Pool which will vary. A starting character begins with 9 Points to distribute as he wishes (e.g. Life 4/4 Will 2/2 Mana 3/3). The Pool will naturally go down during the game, but can also be spent for various bonuses, all based on your character's Occupations(e.g. a pyrokinetic can summon a fire elemental follower). Unless otherwise noted, only one Point can be spent per action. Keep in mind, however, that if any of these Pools is reduced below zero, the Sleeve is exhausted or destroyed, and the Planar must find a new one.


Life represent not only a Sleeves' vitality, but also defensive and restorative capabilities (e.g. armor, regeneration, potions, magic shields etc.).

  • Life Points can be expended to conjure a temporary Follower of modest Occupation (equal to the number of spent Points).

  • Spent to roll again. This can only be done once, on your action, not as a reaction.

Will represent a Sleeves' willpower, stamina, and stress resistance. While a mages’ mind can be particularly strong willed, a Sleeves' physical nerves can only take so much.

  • Can be spent to enhance a spell effect(affecting more targets, a wider area etc.)

  • Will Points can be used to gain a +1 to a single roll.


Mana represent a Sleeves' reserves for magic and equipment. Rather than flat out giving bonuses, purchased equipment usually allows a character to attempt a different sort of action (e.g. wand of levitation).

  • Passable to another player, in whatever form the Planar wishes(Life, Will, Mana).

  • Need some equipment, but forgot to bring it with you? Simply conjure it out in the field by expend Mana Points. Costs may vary, and are usually more expensive than purchasing something with Tass from the market.


In addition, a Sleeve has an three Advantages. These are usually specialized e.g. heavy lifter, multiple arms, acid spitter.


Obtaining a new Sleeve


If a Sleeve is destroyed, there is no actual damage to the soul of the Planar inhabiting it, as long as the phylactery is intact. In fact, the phylactery can be hooked up and merged back with the backup, still available somewhere on a Shardscape server. Whatever form the phylactery takes, it is usually difficult to destroy. If it is destroyed, however, the Planar will not remember anything that happened after the phylactery was attached to the Sleeve. In game terms, this might translate in not gaining as much XP for a particular gaming session.


A new standard Sleeve (9 Points of Traits) costs 1 Tass. For every Trait Maximum and Advantage added, the cost rises by 1 Tass. If a Planar does not have the Tass even for a basic Sleeve or access to a guild which can provide one, there are other ways of obtaining a Sleeve. This usually involves the help of an ally who will find a host (willing but more commonly unwilling) and implant the phylactery in it. Note, however, that these hosts are usually inferior(less than 9 Traits).


Goals and XP


Goals are a character’s personal agenda. They must be specific and achievable (e.g. build an accounting start-up which survives a fiscal year, not get rich). Up to three Goals may be active at any time. Goals are broken up in the actions needed to reach it, and each is a Checkbox. The default number of Checkboxes is 3, though this may vary. When doing something towards fulfilling his Goals, the player should notify the GM. If the GM agrees, he decides how many Checkboxes are ticked since some actions may be more fruitful (e.g. finding a clue about the man who murdered your family nets 1 Checkbox, but killing him might end the Goal). Once a Goal is completed, the character decides on a new one.


When the character completes a Goal, she gets a choice of rewards from the following:

  • 3 Points to replenish any Trait Pool
  • 1 Tass
  • 1 XP

Progressing


Acquiring a new Occupation Rank costs 3 x Desired Rank in XP.(e.g. 15 XP for a +5). Intermediates must also be acquired (e.g. from Rank 1 to Rank 3 costs 18 XP)

Three to One Rule and Target Numbers


At times, your success will depend on how much your Total Result is. For example, when aiding another with an activity, your ally's Total Result increases by 1 for every 3 in your Total Result. This sort of rule is known as Three to One, and can have various forms for various effects e.g. Five to One, One to One.


What follows is a table of Target Numbers to give you an idea of what you may be rolling against, though the GM has final say.

Difficulty  Description     Guidance
0 Routine Any Planar can do this basically every time.
2 Simple Most Planars can do this most of the time.
5 Standard Typical task requiring focus, but most Planars can usually do this.
8 Demanding Requires full attention; most Planars have a 50/50 chance to succeed.
10 Challenging Even trained Planars often fail.
12 Formidable Impossible without skills or great effort.
15 Heroic A task worthy of tales told for years afterward.
20 Nearly Impossible   A task that normal Planars couldn't consider (but one that doesn't break the laws of magic).

Conflicts


As a basic rule, on a successful Result your attacks can harm 1 target for 1 Life. Naturally, this will kill most mortals in one blow, but not all. Various types of equipment and defenses can have various interactions, which will not be detailed, and hopefully be common sense (i.e. punching versus hitting somebody with a sword).


Although magic and common sense don’t exactly mix, keep in mind that while magic is indeed powerful, a body is just as vulnerable to an arrow as it is an acid blast. The context is what matters: who the target is (a fire hound is more vulnerable to an icebolt than an ice djinn), what protection it is using (someone in plate armor won’t care much for flying knives), and environment (no point in casting a fireball underwater).


Planars, usually being experienced in the arts of warfare, have various automatic defenses that spring up when they get attacked. In terms of mechanics, each Planar has an Defense of 5 + relevant Occupation Rank. What form this takes is up to the player.
